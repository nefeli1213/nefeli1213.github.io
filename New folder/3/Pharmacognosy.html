PECTIN:

  • Pectin, is a group of polysaccharides found in nature in the primary cell walls of all seed bearing plants and are invariably located in the middle lamella
  • parent substance Protopectin is insoluble but is easily converted by restricted hydrolysis into pectinic acids (pectins)
  • of d-galacturonic acid residues linked by α-1,4- glycosidic linkages and interspersed with rhamnose units; some of the carboxyl groups are methylate
(Citros limon/aurantium,Ruataceae)
⇒ As Pectin is fairly incompatible with Ca2+, hence due precautions must be taken to avoid the contact of any metallic salts in the course of its preparation
  • richest sources of pectin is lemon or orange rind
  • emulsifying agent and also as a gelling agent preferably in an acidic medium.
  • Poectin in the form of pastes exerts a bacteriostatic activity and hence, is used frequently in the treatment of indolent ulcers and deep wounds

ALAGANIC ACID:

  • Alginic acid, a hard, horny polysaccharide //
  • composition varies according to the biological source but in general is composed of residues of d-mannuronic and l-guluronic acids
  • Alginic acid is the principal constituent of the cell walls of the brown algae
  • Used for the manufacture of alginate salts and fibres (stabilizing, thickening, emulsifying, deflocculating, gelling and film- and filamentforming agents ) — as fibers (eg absorbable haemostatic dressings )
  • composition varies according to the biological source
  • Alginic acid is also used in tablet and liquid preparations for the control of gastro-oesophageal reflux.
  • heteropolyuronide → consisting of chains of β-1,4-linked d-mannuronic acid units → interspersed with lengths of α-1,4-linked l-guluronic acid units together with sections in which the two monouronide units are regularly interspersed.

MUCILAGES:

  • mucilages are generally normal products of metabolism formed within the cell (intracellular formation)
  • storage material, a water-storage reservoir or a protection for germinating seeds
  • often found — epidermal cells of leaves — roots — barks

Glycyrrhizin:

  • a pentacyclic triterpenoid saponin
⇒ LIQUORICE ROOT
  • Glyrrhiza glabra or inflata or uralensis ,Fabaceae
→ about 1.5 m high /papilionaceous flowers of a purplish­blue colour
→ long roots and thin rhizomes
⇒ CONSTITUENTS:
→ sweet taste due to glycyrrhizin, ( potassium and calcium salts of glycyrrhizinic acid)
  • Glycyrrhizinic acid is the di glucopyranosiduronic acid of glycyrrhetic (glycyrrhetinic) acid,
  • yellow colour of liquorice is due to flavonoids (anti-gastric effect)
  • include liquiritin, isoliquiritin (a chalcone, which occurs as a glycoside), liquiritigenin, isoliquiritigenin (chalcone form) and other compounds
  • roots also contain about 5–15% of sugars (glucose, sucrose
  • G lycyrrhizin is confined mainly to the roots but falls rapidly in concentration in organs above soil level
⇒ ACTION AND USES
  • flavouring agent,demulcent,mild expectorant
  • may give symptomatic relief from peptic ulcer pain
  • glycyrrhizin gel can act as a useful vehicle for various drugs used topically and also enhanches skin penetration by the drug.
